Five months

of immersing myself in a different country and culture,
of living with the best roommates I could ask for,
of waking up before dawn to catch the 6:00am train,
of early morning and late afternoon hikes up Arthur’s Seat,
of wandering through the cobblestone streets of this beautiful city,
of castles and cathedrals and charming cities,
of misty mornings and ethereal evenings,
of trying new cuisines and learning new languages,
of making friends from around the globe,

of endless learning and
discovery.

ABOUT

Five months in Scotland, five months on exchange, and five months to explore my interest in travel writing and to pursue a dream of writing a book.

I went on exchange to the University of Edinburgh from January 10th to June 1st, 2018. During these five months, I kept a journal of my daily experiences and adventures, which I later produced as a book. This website aims to share the content of the book in an interactive and hopefully interesting way. I hope you enjoy!

January 10

The Amsterdam airport was very pretty, with a revolving globe showing the location of all the major airports in the world and pretty light fixtures across the airport, as well as a centrally-located bookstore with books in both Dutch and English.

I actually managed to make a friend at the boarding gate as I waited for the flight to Edinburgh — she was traveling alone and looked like a university student, and I was correct when I found out that she was an international first-year student from Nanjing (so she had flown on the same flight from Shanghai as me), returning to Edinburgh for her second semester of first year. We spent the rest of the wait chatting and getting to know each other, and she gave me plentiful tips and advice for my exchange semester. Once we arrived in Edinburgh, we took a taxi into the city together, where she pointed out notable buildings and important landmarks, and finally asked the taxi driver to drop me off first.
January 11

My next stop was the Visiting Students’ Office, where we were all herded into a waiting room and a student ambassador was sent to keep us company while we waited. He was from Pakistan and studied Chemical Engineering, and his enthusiasm for his field was very nice to see. He asked us where we were from and what we were studying and what goals we had for our exchange semester, and even though ultimately I was still sent somewhere else to finish matriculation, I didn’t mind the time that I spent in the waiting room at all.

It turned out that matriculation actually happened at Old College, which was probably the most iconic view of the University of Edinburgh. Unfortunately, a portion of it was currently covered by construction, which really reminded me of UBC. In any case, the part that wasn’t covered up was really pretty and I snapped a couple photos before heading inside with my passport, and the interior was modern and sleek, a stark contrast to the outside.
January 12

Games Night lasted for about an hour before most of us headed off to the Ceilidh that was taking place at Teviot Row House. I ended up walking there with Angela, a girl from Deaconess who I learned studied computer science at Princeton University, and although I was soon separated from the rest of the New Arthur Place residents among the loud music, dark room, and energetic Scottish dancing, I managed to meet Angela’s friends, some of which were also from Princeton while others were from her residence. I stayed at the Ceilidh for quite a number of hours — the dances were fun, reminding me of the dances at Chicoutimi, even though this event was much more chaotic and not as organized. We often ended up with random partners, frequently bumped into one another, and at one point I was almost kicked in the face when a very tall guy lifted his very tall girlfriend and her stretched-out legs barely managed to miss my face.

Normal dance music sometimes interspersed the Scottish dances and we escaped to the bar occasionally for some much-needed water, and it was there that we socialized (away from the deafening music) and got to know each other. Along with two other Princeton students, I also met another student from Queen’s — just meeting another Canadian amongst all these Americans immediately gave us a close bond — and two students from Sydney, Australia.
January 13

I was definitely not ready to get out of bed when my alarm clock went off this morning, and I ended up showing up at the New Arthur Place gate at 9:50am without having eaten any breakfast, which was an excellent start to the day that really reminded me of the time in Québec City when I went on a hike with four hours’ sleep and no food. In any case, we headed off towards Arthur’s Seat, stopping at Deaconess on the way to pick up the residents from that building, and during most of the hike up the mountain, I walked silently and alone, mostly lost in my thoughts while everyone around me chatted and socialized with their friends.

We had quite a bit of time at the top of Arthur's Seat to look around, absorb the view, and take as many photographs as our freezing hands and the freezing wind allowed us. I ran into one of the guys from Hong Kong that I had met yesterday at the PPLS welcome event and his friends all greeted me in Cantonese, which was slightly awkward as I didn’t know whether I should reply in Mandarin or English. Luckily, the hike back down was slightly easier in terms of the route so we quickly escaped the cold, and as we neared the residences, I listened to the conversations around me as people discussed where they wanted to travel to and what they wanted to see while they were on exchange.
January 14

I ended up walking to George Square with the two girls from Beijing University that I had met during Games Night, and we chatted mostly in English — I asked them about growing up in China and about their university and about Beijing — until they asked me if I spoke Mandarin as well, and we switched into Chinese.

Again, I was reminded of the “1.5 syndrome” — when white people doubt if I speak English well and when Chinese people doubt if I speak Chinese well. Even after half a week, I’m still looking for where I belong, where I can find other CBCs (or ABCs or BBCs, depending on where they’re from) like me.
January 15

There were five rounds in total for trivia, beginning with film, and then geography, music, food and drink, and general knowledge. I barely knew any of the titles for the first round, except for the really famous ones such as Beauty and the Beast or It, although I did quite well for the geography round, providing many of the answers in my group — I immediately thought of Oxford for “What is the only town in the UK whose name starts with an ‘o’?” or Rotterdam for “What is the second most populous city in the Netherlands?” I again knew nothing for the last three rounds, but among the eight of us, we could usually come up with a plausible answer for most of the questions, except for the ones that were extremely British-centric.

It was a fun evening — I got to know some of the people that I hadn’t met yet and chatted a bit with those who I already knew, and there was often laughter and fun socializing around the table when we weren’t busy answering questions. A few of them decided to share two pitchers of apple cider, and I tried a sip from Haley’s cup — this was probably an acquired taste that I had yet to acquire, but I was constantly reminding myself to be willing to try new things. At the end of the night, the hosts read out everyone’s team names — the theme was “2018 Headlines,” so a lot of the names were about Trump; ours was Colonies Return Under British Rule, which was amusing because we were all either American, Canadian, or Australian.
January 16

Soon, I headed home, where I was probably supposed to be productive for the rest of the afternoon, except I ended up chatting with Stephanie and Nick for the next three hours in one of my most entertaining conversations thus far in Scotland. I was munching on my dinner during it, making myself spinach-hummus wraps and nibbling on apples and yogurt as well, although it took me much longer than usual to eat, because we we were all laughing so hard.

It was particularly amusing because I was Canadian, Nick was American, and Stephanie was British, and even though we deceptively all spoke English (with another couple of languages for Stephanie and me), there were more than enough differences in our language and culture to entertain us for hours on end. We compared the imperial and metric systems, where Stephanie and I both agreed that our system was much better than the American system, or the side of the road that one drove on, where Nick and I were in agreement. We talked about the ground floor not being the first floor — again, North America differed from Britain — or the water taps.

At one point, we were talking about the temperature, which was always a fun topic. Stephanie declared that she considered a comfortable temperature anything above ten degrees, and I offered that I preferred temperatures of fifteen degrees and up. “I like seventy-two and up,” Nick added, completely poker-faced, while Stephanie and I burst into laughter. At another point, Nick began, “You know what I hate about this country?” to which Stephanie exclaimed, “Rude!” I suggested, “You know what I’m finding it hard to get used to?” and Stephanie approved, looking pointedly at Nick, “That’s how you start a sentence.”
January 17

After class, I ended up chatting with Weijia, the girl from Fudan, along with a Master’s student at Edinburgh that I had met on Monday, because it turned out that I had all three of my classes with her. Our conversation quickly switched into Chinese (much to Weijia’s surprise) and we chatted about our linguistics courses and the professors and the differences between university in Europe and in China. Eventually, the three of us headed out of the building together towards our next destinations, and after parting ways with the Master’s student, Weijia and I continued walking together and chatting about our experiences so far, including how Edinburgh was different from China, how many Americans we were meeting, and how many of the Americans of Asian heritage couldn’t speak their heritage languages anymore.

Soon I parted ways with her, because I wanted to check out the Give It A Go Fair, which was equivalent to UBC’s Club Fair. It was in Potterrow, and I wandered around alone for a bit — I found LangSoc, a couple Engineering-based clubs, and some business-related ones. In The Venue, I found some of the cultural clubs, and I signed up for the Exchange club as well as the International Students’ Centre mailing list, and an interesting-sounding Third Culture Kid Club — technically, I didn’t fit the exact definition of a TCK, but I was often lost enough that I could probably relate to their struggles of belonging and identity.
January 18

After a couple rounds of the drinking game, Nick came home from soccer at 9:30pm, and despite Alyssa’s insistence, he declined her invitation to go out with us tonight. He hung out with us in the kitchen for a bit, telling us about his soccer practice tonight, although soon it was time for us to leave; the pub that we were visiting tonight was Greenmantle, just across from Tesco.

The four of us walked there together, chatting along the way, although once we arrived, we were all surprised at how full and crowded it was — this was definitely a popular pub, especially on Thursday nights when the ISC had their weekly pub nights.
January 19

I made a nametag for myself and found some people to talk to; the first person I met spoke English and Mandarin and was from Singapore, and when I asked him what he studied, he told me that he was in his fifth year of chemical engineering.

It turned out that most people at this Speed Lingua session were STEM students, which was unsurprisingly because Kings Buildings was the location for the science and engineering schools — attending an event like this would expose me to people that I probably wouldn’t get to meet on the central campus.
January 20

I even ended up sharing with Ashwin some thoughts that I’ve been ruminating on for the past few months when he asked me what I wanted to do with linguistics and French after graduation — I told him about education, but I also told him about computational linguistics, and we talked about what a technology-centered career path would look like. He encouraged me to pursue what made me happy (I told him about how weird it was that my parents, who both had engineering backgrounds, ended up with an Arts child), although he agreed that programming and coding were just useful skills to have in general, and he maintained that literally anyone could contribute something to artificial intelligence and machine learning, no matter their background, because so many people would be required to work on a project of this scale. Even Philosophy majors had something to contribute, he said as an example, and I immediately burst out, “Ethics!” because that was a huge part of artificial intelligence.

There was barely a pause in our conversation as we chatted about our fields of study and the places in which we had grown up and languages in general and what we aimed to do in the future; by the time our conversation showed any signs of slowing down, it was already almost 5:00pm, the official end of the event. Ashwin ended up having to leave because he had other obligations for the evening, although I hoped that I would see him again soon; I hadn’t had such a satisfying conversation in probably really long, and I was immensely glad for this one.
January 21

Soon, the bus grew quiet as the sky grew light outside, and I lost myself in my thoughts as we sped down the highway, towards the Scottish highlands. There was some conversation in the bus, although they were conducted in murmured whispers so as to not wake those who were sleeping; I was content to remain in silence, too tired to talk but too stubborn to sleep. Occasionally, the overhead speakers would crackle to life when the tour guide had something to announce — when we passed through Glasgow, our tour guide pointed out some of the buildings that we could see from the highway and told us about a few more that we should see when we properly visited Glasgow, and our bus driver gave his opinion on why people from Glasgow were better than people from Edinburgh.

We also passed Loch Lomond, and as we sped past the icy banks of the famous lake, most of us woke up to stare outside at the white landscape — white sky, white lake, white snow. Our tour guide also played the famous Loch Lomond song, which always reminds me of my high school flute days when I learned this song in one of my flute books, and explained the story behind it. Finally, we arrived at our first stop for the morning, a viewpoint called Rest and Be Thankful. It was freezing and small snowflakes were drifting from the skies as we all hopped out of the bus, and everything around us was white — the snow-covered trees and mountains rather reminded me of Canadian winter scenery.
January 22

The banker told me that I had come at a fortunate time, because they were empty enough at the moment that he could help me immediately without having to book an appointment for me (I could barely believe my luck). He went through the procedures of opening an account for me, giving me terms and conditions to read through and documents to sign, and made copies of my various documents. He was extremely nice and friendly, too, with an easy-to-understand English accent; he asked me what I studied, why I had chosen Edinburgh, and what my life back in Canada was like. We talked about the difference between Canadians and Americans, and when I mentioned at one point that some Americans pretend to be Canadians so that people are nicer to them, he laughed, “I can believe that.”
January 23

My conversation with Noah was a bit longer; he asked, referring to a conversation that I had with Nick yesterday night, if my laptop was really in French, asking if French was my first language. I told him that it was Mandarin, and that I was Chinese, although he still seemed to struggle slightly with the idea that my laptop was in my third language and I had willingly chosen to set it to French.

As he continued preparing his breakfast, we chatted about our accommodations; I mentioned that this was a very new experience for me because I had never lived with Americans before, with white people before, or with guys before. Noah laughed that I was getting all three experiences at once, and added that he had never lived with someone like me before — “An Asian nerd?” I interjected.

“No,” he replied, “I don’t stereotype like that,” adding that he had never lived with someone who was so much smarter than him.
January 24

We parted ways as we neared campus, and I headed to Potterrow, where there was a sort of market going on today. It was a bit smaller than I had expected, as I entered the main floor — there were a number of stalls set up under the Dome, and I wandered around, looking at all the offerings from vendors around the city.

There was lots of cool food, ranging from a cheese stall to a paella stall to tempting-looking pastries and bread products — I really wanted to try one of the pastries, but I figured that I should actually buy a proper lunch instead, so I ended up purchasing two slices of a Middle Eastern chickpea pie; one was a cauliflower slice and the other a zucchini slice.
January 25

“I’m sorry, but that’s a bit nerdy,” he told me.

“Oh it’s okay, I’m definitely nerdy,” I replied cheerfully.
January 26

We headed downstairs for drinks — everyone ordered something except for me, and I stared cautiously at the slightly-too-full glasses of beer and other drinks and hoped that nobody would spill their drinks on me — and socialized and danced a bit in the overly-loud room. We returned to the main floor at exactly midnight, because there was supposed to be a Frankenstein that rose to life — everyone paused their dancing and whipped out their phones to take photos or videos or Snapchats, as Frankenstein descended from the ceiling, looked around, emitted some words in a low, deep voice, spewed out green smoke, and then ascended into the ceiling again. It was almost kind of disappointing — I had been expecting something perhaps slightly more. The robotic nature of this Frankenstein had been slightly too artificial.

Afterwards, everyone resumed dancing and drinking, and I attempted to pretend that I didn’t feel totally out of place even though I totally was, and we might have stayed in Frankenstein for much longer, except at some point, we realized that Sid and Zoe had disappeared; they had said that they would be back five minutes ago, and much more time than that had elapsed, and there was no sign of them.
January 27

Just down the street from the museum, however, was Greyfriars Kirk, and they mentioned that the inspiration for some of the names in Harry Potter had been taken from the gravestones in Greyfriars Kirk, so we decided to check it out.

The wind was still strong and the ground muddy beneath our feet, but we trekked through the graveyard, stopping occasionally to take pictures or peer at the half-worn-away inscriptions on some of the gravestones.
January 28

The bus stopped in Stonehaven, where we had a bit more than an hour to wander and explore around Dunnottar Castle. Now that it was actually sunny and bright, even the fiercely-blowing wind couldn’t deter us from admiring the gorgeous landscape — the castle, which didn’t seem to bear too much resemblance to the castle in Brave but was pretty nonetheless, stood on a sort of cliff overlooking the ocean, which spread into the distance. We headed down the path towards the castle, although we turned left, towards the War Memorial, for our first stop, anticipating that most people would head straight for the castle as it was the main attraction.

We ended up not making it to the War Memorial because the perfect Instagram spot was located halfway between the War Memorial and the castle, and we carefully made our way through the thick, soft grass until we reached a cliff that overlooked the inlet — on one side we could see the blue sky with wisps of white cloud, a sandy beach far below, and cliffs that stretched out into the sea; on the other side we could see the castle and the sun.
January 29

The other very interesting conversation of the night focused on frat parties, because Nick and I had been chatting about the portrayal of frat parties in media and he had been protesting that they weren’t actually as bad as they seemed. We asked Noah about this, because he was obviously the one with the most experience, and instead of directly answering my question, he said simply, “What do you think they’re like?”

Nick looked at me pointedly, and I hesitated for a moment before giving a few general comments based on what I had heard and knew — they were loud, full of people and alcohol, and sometimes the guys were uncomfortably grabby. Noah acknowledged most of my answers, although in response to the last point he said that in his fraternity, any guy caught being sexually aggressive would be kicked out of the frat, although he admitted that in other fraternities that might be a larger problem.
January 30

We chatted about our classes along the way, and Weijia told me about studying English in China, which she had done for her undergraduate degree before going to Fudan for her Master’s — she sighed that they were at a great disadvantage compared to students who studied English at an anglophone institution, because English for them was a foreign language while for us was our native language, which meant that even at a second-year level, she explained, they only had the English proficiency of a sixth-grader.

I related to that sentiment a lot, telling her that it was the same situation for us when we studied a foreign language as our undergraduate specialization — I was still very aware that the more time I spent without speaking French, the worse it was steadily becoming.
January 31

Nick headed off to sleep soon after finishing cooking, announcing that the chef didn’t wash dishes. Stephanie and I ended up staying in the kitchen for a while longer — the French toast was quite good, but I wanted some more food, so we decided to share some kale chips along with the rest of my grapes.

It was a pleasant night together, as we bonded over food and conversation about everything ranging from our families to American stereotypes to our opinions on cross-cultural relationships. By the time we were both ready to leave the kitchen, even my headache had almost completely disappeared, which I was extremely grateful for.

February 1

My next event of the night started at 5:00pm, so I quickly headed to the Psychology building, finding the correct lecture hall for the Beyond Your Degree event just before the first speaker was about to start. It was very similar to the Beyond the BA events that SALSA held at UBC, although this was an event for the entire PPLS school, which meant that the speakers were graduates from not only Linguistics, but also Psychology, Philosophy, and Cognitive Science. They held jobs ranging from advertising to cybersecurity to consulting, and some of them were from the UK while others were from elsewhere, and each one gave good advice, from staying curious to staying focused and motivated and not feeling alone in our uncertainty. There were no epiphanies on my end, although I did hope for a bit of reassurance.

Afterwards, we had an hour to just network with the speakers and with the other attendees and nibble on some refreshments that the school had provided, and I managed to chat with one of the speakers who had turned her Cognitive Science degree into an IT job; she talked about gaining experience in technology without having a technical degree and emphasized the application of the skills that we learned in our humanities and social sciences degrees — we studied humanity and how humans functioned and we were used to writing papers and explaining and arguing and defending our position, and these were all important skills to have.
February 2

After class, I ended up leaving the building with Weijia and the two other Masters students that she had been talking to earlier, and the other two girls wanted to grab lunch together and invited Weijia and me to come along. The establishment that they were going to was a Thai restaurant just on the other side of the street from the Medical School (sometimes the idea of the campus being in the middle of the city was still a little strange, because I still wasn’t used to being able to so easily access the city from the school), and Weijia commented that the last time that she had been here, it had been extremely crowded.

The four of us managed to find a table, and one of the girls wanted a meal all to herself while the remaining three of us decided to share a pad thai. Conversation, as we waited for our food and then ate together, was entirely in Chinese and very fun, ranging from the differences between universities here and in China (when the other two girls heard that Weijia was from Fudan, they were suitably impressed, as I had been when I had first found out) and where we were planning to travel to during our exchange here. I was also asked about growing up in Canada, and why my parents were currently living in Shanghai when I had grown up in Vancouver.
February 3

I had invited Ashwin for lunch because I had wanted to chat and have a conversation like the first one that we had had two weeks ago, so I promptly announced that I had been going to all the NLP classes so far this semester, and he asked me how they were going.

We talked about the topics covered in NLP, its relation to linguistics (he said that it was the least computer-science-y course in the department, for me it was definitely the most computer-science-y course I’ve ever taken at university), and how far I’d want to pursue this.
February 4

We each had a brush with us to spend some time with the ponies, cleaning them and letting them get used to our presence, and even though the ground was thick with mud and my boots made a squelching sound every time I took a step, we approached the ponies and began running our brushes down their flanks. I murmured to the pony that I was with in soft Chinese, and hoped that this pony trekking experience would be more comfortable and relaxed than my last horseback riding experience.

I was with a pony named Bailey, and one of the tour guides and I chatted as we headed off, talking about everything from what we studied, to why I had chosen to come to Edinburgh, to what life was like in Canada and what the city of Vancouver was known for. We headed through the Pentlands, trotting occasionally (which wasn’t an entirely comfortable feeling, especially as I bounced up and down on the pony), and I tried to admire the pretty landscape when I wasn’t trying to prevent Bailey from stopping by the side of the trail to nibble on the tall grass.

February 5

I happily wandered around Blackwell's for a bit — I started upstairs, where a section titled, “Tomorrow’s world?” caught my eye, and I flipped through a few titles. They ranged from topics such as big data (and the lack of privacy on the internet) to China in the twenty-first century, which offered quite a few thought-provoking ideas on the country’s politics, economy, and future.

I also stopped by the computing section, where I flipped through some books on machine translation and artificial intelligence, before descending to the lowest floor to find the books that I had actually come for — the travel books, because I wanted to plan for my London trip and dream about the rest of the trips that I was going to take. I found a handbook on solo traveling which I flipped through, reading about safety tips and ways to meet new people when traveling solo and important things to keep in mind — my trip was just going to be a smaller taster of solo traveling, because I wasn’t even leaving the country and I was only going for about five days, although it would be a good lesson in independence, confidence, and, hopefully, self-discovery.
February 6

Just outside the library, I spotted Ian; Weijia and I parted ways, and Ian and I greeted each other, heading off in the direction of the Chinese supermarket that I needed to visit. We chatted about our classes and our assignments as we made our way down the street, and I told him about the Colombian dinner that Stephanie had cooked for us last night, including all the food (and the sangria) that she had made. We talked about what we wanted to cook tonight — I shared with him some of my ideas, and he told me what he wanted to contribute to the dinner. It had been a while since either of us had cooked, but we were both very excited for tonight.

It didn’t take long before we reached the Chinese supermarket, and we headed inside to browse around; Ian mostly followed me. I started off with the main dishes, quickly finding bai guo, green onion pancakes, and tangyuan, choosing both sesame and red bean flavours (I actually liked peanut flavour the best, but Noah was allergic to nuts). Ian and I also bought a couple different kinds of drinks, including aloe vera (which I hadn’t had in a while) and three different drinks of his choice, including a mango juice, a Brazilian soft drink that he cheerfully described as “artificial ingredients and chemicals,” and a Korean soybean drink.
February 7

We chatted quite a bit with Antonella, as if in office hours but without the office. Some people asked her more questions about her talk, and we chatted about bilingualism outreach, immersion schools (she mentioned Gaelic immersion schools in Scotland while I thought of French immersion schools in Canada), and what kind of research people conducted in this field. Antonella could only stay for ten minutes, so she soon left, but the rest of us stayed for a bit longer, and I got to know Tonje’s friend better — I learned that she was from Innsbruck, Austria, and spoke German and a couple other languages. Along with Tonje, we chatted about travel around Europe (Tonje laughed that she should travel more, because travel around Europe was so convenient, but she often didn’t feel the motivation to leave her city), linguistics in general, and the classes we were taking here.
February 8

The other three soon returned; Ian had a vodka lemonade, the same drink that we had shared at Greenmantle last week. We had originally come to this pub for their trivia night, but apparently there was a darts tournament occurring tonight that took the place of the weekly trivia night, so we ended up just chatting and drinking together.

There were two large TV screens on either end of the pub, and they were currently showing a professional darts match, which was almost comical in how serious and professional it was, as huge crowds, cheering wildly and bearing signs and other items to support their favourite players, gathered to watch two, middle-aged-looking men throw darts at a target.
February 9

Noah was hosting an “authentic American frat party” tonight, and Ian was already in our unit by the time I came back. Soon, there were quite a number of people clustered around our small little kitchen table — Noah, Betsy, Ian, Martina, Fabian, Sid, me, and Alyssa — and a variety of alcohol available.

I was asked multiple times why I wasn’t drinking, although I was still participating in the games with my water bottle, and, perhaps surprisingly and perhaps not, Noah was the one who most vocally defended me, declaring that I didn’t need to drink alcohol if I didn’t want to. I was grateful towards him, and glad that he was playing such a major role in breaking the stereotypes of frat boys that I had been exposed to throughout my years at university.
February 10

The walking tour was an hour and a half long, and we stopped at a number of places, some that I recognized and some that I didn’t — we didn’t venture across North Bridge and mostly stayed near the Royal Mile, although at times we ducked into closes or passed through more dark alleyways (where I tried not to trip on the uneven cobblestone) and at one point even entered a graveyard for more spooky stories. Our tour guide, who was very animate and never hesitated to imitate the screams and shrieks with as much passion and fervour as the originals must have, told us all manners of stories, ranging from the witch hunts that went on in Edinburgh (this reminded me vaguely of high school history classes) to cannibal clans to evil fairies to grave robbers.

Although the stories were interesting (and reminded me of all the reasons why we should be grateful that we didn’t live in the Edinburgh of the past), situations were slightly awkward or uncomfortable at times, because we were always in public and therefore passerby and pedestrians constantly passed our tour group. The most uncomfortable situation occurred under North Bridge when a man attempted to pick a fistfight with our tour guide, and I stepped back just slightly so that I was closer to my friends because this was actually a scary situation, but luckily a couple other men in the tour group stepped forward to diffuse the situation before it could escalate any further.

The rest of the awkward moments beyond that were of a much tamer nature — at one point, the tour guide was mimicking the screams of a man possessed by the devil during one of his witch hunt stories, and a passerby politely asked him if he was okay and if he needed any help. Our tour guide replied, in a totally calm and normal voice, that he was fine and was just giving a tour, and as soon as the passerby continued on, he resumed screaming about the devil.
February 11

The SpeakEasy event was somewhat like the Coffeehouse events in Marine Drive, and I had always said that I would participate in one but never quite had the courage to do so. Tonight, I decided to share one of my stories, because I knew that I would regret it if I didn’t seize this chance, and I was in the middle of a room full of strangers; only Daniel, and now Karis and Aayush, knew who I was. I put my name in the hat, reminded myself that I had used to give speeches all the time at Toastmasters, and when my name was drawn, I took a deep breath and walked up to the stage.

May 13

Florence reminded me of Lyon in many ways, and the gardens and hills and greenery on the other side of the river was just one characteristic that the two cities shared. Ponte Vecchio, unsurprisingly, was full of people, but the buildings that hugged the side of the bridge were quite unique — they seemed to be attached to the bridge the way a treehouse would be nestled in a tree. I took my time crossing it, passing by souvenir shops and groups of tourists with cameras and selfie sticks, and stopped for a few moments to admire the view of the bridges downriver which spanned the length of the very yellow Arno.

Despite the thick clouds that were now rolling in, it was a beautiful piazza — the basilica itself was an impressive sight, of course, but the thick border of bright pink flowers which lined the piazza added a splash of vibrancy to the scene that I particularly liked. A man with a guitar and a crooning voice was giving a musical performance in the centre of the piazza, and judging from the size of the crowd that he had attracted, his performance was quite remarkable.
May 14

I finally arrived at the top of the bell tower after about fifteen minutes of climbing, and from a height of about eighty or so meters, my view of Florence was almost as good as the view from the top of the dome. Although large swathes of the city were still covered by the shadows of clouds, it was already much brighter than it had been earlier, and I could see for quite a distance in all directions.

I slowly made my way around the entire top of the tower, lingering in every direction to admire the sheer prettiness of the Florentine rooftops and the mountains and parks in the distance. I picked out all the sights that I recognized, from the Palazzo Vecchio to the Arno to Piazzale Michelangelo to the Uffizi; in another direction, I spotted the Santa Maria Novella and the central market (it appeared even larger from this height) and the train station. Ordinary buildings were packed quite densely together, although a number of them featured balconies and rooftop patios and what appeared to be communal areas, and I imagined that on a sunny day, they’d be packed with the residents of the building. Looking around, I couldn’t help thinking once more that Florence reminded me so much of Lyon.

The streets were brightly-illuminated, lighting up the buildings by the side of the road in a sort of golden colour, and I walked slowly in the direction of the Arno, admiring the nighttime scene. I soon reached the bridge, and the view from the middle of the bridge, looking out over the water, was even more beautiful than I had expected — the reflections of the streetlights along the river rippled gently in the water, while the faintest hues of orange and yellow were still barely visible on the western horizon. I stood in the center of the bridge for a few minutes, admiring the scene and wishing that I had left the market a bit earlier to admire the sunset, which would have been undoubtedly gorgeous.
May 15

Luckily, a couple blocks away from the train station, I found another gelato establishment (it was Italy, after all — gelato shops abounded), and decided to head in. I chose two nutty flavours for my last gelato in Italy, pistachio and hazelnut, and the small cup of gelato came with a wafer on top. I took small spoonfuls of the sweet gelato as I walked the final few blocks to the train station, savouring the flavours — I decided that these two flavours were my favourite gelato flavours of all the ones that I had tasted in Italy.

May 16

This gave me over an hour to fully enjoy the sunshine and take a walk in the Meadows, which I had also recently wanted to do; cherry blossom season was already reaching its end, but there were still a few pink petals clinging to the trees that lined the paths through the Meadows, and I wanted to admire them before the wind blew the last of them onto the ground. Nonetheless, I continued my walk through the Meadows, watching showers of cherry blossom petals gently drift to the ground — Nick had called this “fairy dust,” and the name seemed appropriate for the shimmering pink petals. There were entire flowers that had dropped onto the ground, and they looked as if they had just recently fallen — the petals hadn’t wilted yet — so I decided to collect a small handful of them to bring home. I didn’t know what I wanted to do with them, but perhaps they could be pretty somewhere.

Even though I hadn’t even known about the Norwegian national day before this year, and even though I had known nothing about Scottish-Norwegian relations, I felt as if a lot of the content of the presentations was very relatable because, in some way, it was so universal.

May 22

Roe sighed that the international image of Norway, at least online, was that it was such a fairytale, dreamlike country with no flaws, and Tonje gave quite an in-depth and thorough explanation of why this image didn’t reflect current political situations in Norway. She admitted that Norway in many aspects was an excellent place to live with a high quality of life — she had earlier mentioned wanting to take summer courses and “getting her university to pay for them,” and I had burst out, “I wish I could get my university to pay for my courses!” (to which she had replied, “Are courses expensive?” and I had grumbled, “Everything’s expensive”).

May 23

I probably could have stayed in the Baltic for the entire day, but I still wanted to see the rest of Newcastle, so I left the art centre at 4:00pm with about four hours before my train back to Edinburgh.

I headed across the Millennium Bridge back into Newcastle, where I wandered through a maze of twisting, sloping streets until I reached Grey Street, which was supposedly the “finest street in Britain,” according to an online vote — the architecture was pretty stunning, elegantly lining the road, but I didn’t necessarily think that it was that much more impressive than some of the streets in Edinburgh’s New Town. Perhaps it was the thick layer of clouds that reduced everything to dull, muted colours that influenced my impression.
May 24

The next room was devoted to the old town, and in particular I really liked a model of the old town portraying what it would have looked like a couple hundred years ago; it showed the Royal Mile running from Edinburgh Castle to the Palace of Holyroodhouse, and St. Giles Cathedral, among other buildings, was clearly recognizable. Other exhibits surrounding the model focused on aspects such as sanitation (or, rather, the lack thereof) or industry in the old town.

A couple rooms further into the museum, I found the rooms devoted to the history of the new town — I learned that Princes Street Gardens had used to be a moat protecting Edinburgh Castle before it had turned into the public park that it was today, and I admired the maps that sketched out the layout of the streets of the new town. The names chosen for the streets were explained as well — Princes Street, George Street, and Queen Street obviously referenced the monarchy, while Rose Street and Thistle Street referenced the national plants of England and Scotland. On the walls were hung a few paintings depicting the new town as well; they were filled with straight lines and ninety-degree angles, and it was easy to see why they were considered so elegant.
May 25

The other exhibit, located near the end of the museum, focused on the symbolism of the poppy and its significance — I read the poem, “In Flanders Fields,” which was written on the wall, and glanced curiously at the display of different poppies from countries around the world. I hadn’t realized that not all poppies resembled the Canadian poppy that I was used to seeing (I did think ours was the prettiest, though), and, perhaps naively, I hadn’t realized that not all countries associated the poppy with remembering the war in this way. Haile had never heard of the tradition, and I explained to her what Remembrance Day meant in Canada, and how we had always received poppies at school and attended Remembrance Day assemblies during elementary school and high school. It was such a normal part of growing up in Canada that it was almost strange to imagine that this was unfamiliar to someone who had grown up in another country.

Then, Noah gave me a tight hug (it was our first and last hug, and I realized that his hugs were really good), and he told me that he’d miss me: “I’ll miss feeling stupid that you speak seven languages and I speak only one,” he added (I wanted to protest that I definitely did not speak seven languages, but I figured that by this point I probably couldn’t convince him anymore).
May 26

Tucked away in the middle of the gardens was a little fountain and house that I had missed during my exploration earlier. Haile suggested checking out the inside of the small house, and the temporary exhibition currently taking place ended up being one of my absolute favourite parts of the entire gardens, matched only by the gorgeous colours of the azalea lawn.

The introduction to the exhibition stated that children were spending too much time indoors, in front of screens and games instead of outside playing in nature, and they were losing their ability to recognize even basic species of animals and plants. The exhibit aimed to reintroduce the magic of nature through watercolour paintings and nature poetry, featuring these words that “had been lost from the vocabulary of children.” I immediately fell in love as soon as I stepped into the first room of the exhibition — the delicate but richly-painted watercolours were gorgeous, reminding me of the high school years that I had spent painting and the animals that had constituted a majority of my subjects.

The nature poems that accompanied the paintings were brilliant as well — they were meant to be read aloud, almost as if spells, but it was silent in the art exhibition so I simply mouthed the words to myself, imagining how powerful and filled with magic they would sound if spoken aloud. I loved the rhythm and the rhymes, the words bursting with imagery and imagination — they reminded me of William Blake and poetry analyses in ArtsOne, but in a good way. My favourite poem was Kingfisher, which opened with the line, “Kingfisher: the colour-giver, fire-bringer, flame-flicker, river’s quiver” — each word seemed to crackle with energy.

Daniel and I started heading in the direction of the University of Edinburgh, because that seemed like a good place to start, despite the fog. Unfortunately, when I realized that we couldn’t even see a church at the end of the road — “You’d see a church over there,” I told Daniel, pointing at a uniform gray mass of nothingness, “if it were visible” — I suggested that we spend the day in a museum instead. May 28

Daniel and I started heading up the trail that wound up the park to Arthur’s Seat, and even before we had started the climb, the views were already amazing — I pointed out Edinburgh Castle and the old town in the distance, silhouetted against the sky. Because it was vacation season and because the weather was so uncharacteristically impeccable today, the trail was packed with people; most of them seemed to be visitors, judging from the plethora of languages and accents that I heard. Daniel and I stopped occasionally on the way up so that he could admire the view and take photos — Leith and the water didn’t take long to come into sight — and even I was busy absorbing the views, realizing that this was my last climb up Arthur’s Seat, at least for this year.

The climb wasn’t particularly long or difficult, especially considering some of the mountains back home in Vancouver, although I hadn’t climbed up Arthur’s Seat in more than a month and I realized that I hadn’t actually climbed up Arthur’s Seat itself very many times over the semester; many of my adventures in Holyrood Park had been restricted to Salisbury Crags or the lochs, which were much more accessible than the peak of the park. We remained there for over an hour — contemplating a city from its highest point seemed to be my preferred way to say farewell to a city, considering that I had visited Fourvière for over an hour during my last days in Lyon — and I thought about my memories from this semester. I looked around at all the tourists and visitors passing through Edinburgh for a few days, and, as I had done a few weeks ago, thought about how lucky I was that I wasn’t one of them. Edinburgh wasn’t just a stop on the itinerary, a dot on the map — in these last five months, I had made it one of my homes.

UNE PASSION POUR LE VOYAGE, UN RÊVE POUR LA VIE

Une petite réflexion dans l’autobus en route de Berlin à Prague

En achetant mes billets d’avion, je me suis demandée ce qui m’attirait au voyage. Pourquoi passais-je autant de journées que possible en voyageant de pays en pays ? C’est vrai que les images de ces villes m’ont été toujours familières, depuis que j’ai commencé à rêver du voyage. Des grands places dont les bâtiments imposants témoignent des décennies, peut-être des centaines d’années d’histoire. Des rues et des boulevards dont les noms rappellent tant de cours d’histoire et de langues, des chansons et des romans et des films. Des plats locaux qui ne se trouvent pas chez moi. Des gens, les individus qui résident dans ces villes, que je n’aurais jamais rencontrés, dont les vies sont peut-être semblables à la mienne, ou peut-être complètement différentes.

Le voyage, c’est la richesse. C’est l’occasion d’explorer, d’apprendre, de se sentir inspiré. Réinventé, peut-être. De se lancer dans l’inconnu, pourtant ce n'est peut-être pas la destination qu’on trouve à la fin, mais soi-même. De voir avec ses propres yeux à ce qui ressemble le monde, de se rendre compte qu'à quel point le monde est énorme, illimité. De se rendre compte, peut-être, qu'à quel point le voyageur est illimité.

Après plus de cinq jours de voyage, j’ai découvert cinq raisons pour lesquelles l’envie de voyager est tellement forte.

Un— Je veux vraiment connaitre un nouveau coin du monde.

Si on m’a demandé ce que je savais sur la ville de Copenhague avant que je m'y sois rendue, je n’aurais pu dire que, « C’est la capitale du Danemark. » Mais après les quarante-huit heures que j’ai passé dans cette ville de taille moyenne, Copenhague est devenu beaucoup plus qu’une ville sur la carte du monde accroché au mur dans ma chambre. Je sais maintenant qu’on peut trouver, dans le halle ouest de Torvehallerne, un étal qui vend des smørrebrød qui coutent un peu chers mais qui sont toujours délicieux, un festin pour les yeux autant que pour les papilles. Je sais maintenant qu’à 22h45 dans les jardins de Tivoli commence un spectacle appelé Illuminations, pendant lequel l’eau, la fumée, la lumière et le feu éblouissent l’audience ravi qui se rassemblent à côté du lac. Je sais également qu’il faut s’y rendre au moins cinq minutes à l’avance pour garantir la vue la plus spectaculaire. Enfin, je sais que le magasin Illums Bolighus est sans doute l’un des magasins les plus beaux que j’aie vus dans ma vie, car le magasin n’était pas qu’un magasin, mais c’était un véritable oeuvre d’art.

Deux— Je veux vivre des histoires.

« Ton voyage s’est bien passé ? » me demandera sûrement un ami qui s’attendra à une réponse de « Oui, mon voyage s’est bien passé. » Mais comment pourrais-je résumer des semaines ou bien des mois en une seule phrase ? Une réponse de « oui » ne signifie rien. Mon carnet de voyage sera rempli d’histoires et de photos, de petits moments magiques et de grandes leçons importantes. Le currywurst que j’ai savouré dans un parc berlinois à côté de la rivière pendant un après-midi ensoleillé, après presque une semaine de journées pluvieuses consécutives. Alexanderplatz à Berlin à 21h00, où j’ai regardé flâner et bavarder et rire tous les gens autour de moi pendant que la tour TV surgissait dans le ciel nocturne comme une lune artificielle. J’étais contente, à l’aise, calme. J’aimais l’atmosphère, l'énergie, la musique que jouait quelqu’un avec une guitare, les bruits d’une place pleine de vie. Les lumières de la tour TV, la fontaine. Les gens surtout. Les clients, les couples, les amis, les familles. Les voyageurs, peut-être, des voyageurs comme moi.

Trois— Je veux rencontrer des gens qui proviennent des tous les coins du monde.

Pendant mon premier voyage en solo, dans le train à destination de Londres, je me suis trouvée à côté d’un passager britannique qui était en chemin pour rendre visite à sa famille. Nous nous sommes mis à converser et j’ai appris qu’il avait étudié à UCL il y a quelques décennies et que quelques membres de sa famille habitaient à Vancouver, dans un quartier à côté de celui-ci où j’avais grandi. Dans mon auberge de jeunesse à Copenhague, la femme qui dormait dans le lit au-dessous du mien travaillait à Chicago et avait étudié à Barcelone pendant ses études universitaires, et elle avait décidé de passer du temps au Danemark, aux Pays-Bas et en Belgique pour s’échapper de sa vie ordinaire pendant quelques semaines. Dans la croisière du Danemark en Allemagne à 1h00, j’ai appris que l’étudiant qui s’asseyait derrière moi avait grandi dans l’un de mes quartiers préférés à Shanghai, et qu’il souhaitait déménager à Montréal après ses études d'architecture. Je ne sais pas comment s’appelait ces gens, car ils m’ont raconté seulement leurs histoires, mais parfois ce sont les histoires et pas les noms qui restent dans la mémoire.

Quatre— Je veux prendre de belles photos.

La photographie, c’est un art, malgré les foules de touristes qui s’attroupent devant des monuments pour prendre des photos identiques, avant de monter dans le bus touristique pour visiter le prochain monument. Une bonne photo ne montre pas qu’à quoi ressemble l’objet de la photo, mais elle montre la façon dont le photographe le perçoit. De plus, l’art a toujours fait une grande partie de ma vie. C’est pour cela que j’ai attendu pendant une trentaine de minutes devant la porte Brandenburg le moment parfait où le soleil couchant s’apparait entre les arches de la porte. C’est pour cela que j’ai grimpés les quatre cents marches jusqu’au sommet de l’église de Notre-Sauveur à Copenhague, afin de voir un vue spectaculaire sur toute la ville. C’est pour cela que j’adore les façades colorées de tous les bâtiments dans la capitale danoise, parce que je connais très peu de villes qui appartiennent aussi sûrement au monde des contes de fées.

Cinq— Je veux me sentir complètement libre.

C’est probablement vrai que l’idée de courir des dizaines de kilomètres dans des villes munies de transports publics de haute qualité est un peu folle. Mais je ne veux pas me rendre simplement d’une destination A jusqu’à une destination B, parce que les découvertes les plus mémorables se cachent souvent sur le chemin. Sur une rue dans un quartier de Berlin qui apparaissait assez banal sur la carte, j’ai trouvé par hasard la plus jolie libraire dans toute la ville, un petit magasin rempli de plantes en pot, de lumières brillantes et de chansons anglais pop qui vend des lithographies aussi créatives que l’apparence du magasin, et d’innombrables cafés et boulangeries dont les viennoiseries et les petits gâteaux m’ont fait oublier que je venais juste d’acheter mon déjeuner.

Dans l’autobus à destination de Prague, j’ai hâte de découvrir les surprises que la ville me révélera. Malgré le sommeil qui me manque, la lourdeur de mon sac à dos et l’épuisement qui se répand dans mon corps après avoir marché plus de quarante kilomètres pendant une seule journée, l’appel de voyage est toujours plus fort.
